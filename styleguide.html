Foundation
The App

Every design decision in this system flows from a clear understanding of what Pensee is, who it is for, and what it believes. Read this section before building anything.

Core proposition
"AI is writing for a generation that is forgetting how. Pensee is built for the ones who want to remember."

What Pensee is. Pensee is a daily writing practice app. Each morning it gives the user one carefully selected literary quote, a ten-minute countdown timer, and three rhetorical or vocabulary tools woven into the prompt. The session ends. The writing is saved. The user returns tomorrow. That is the entire loop.

It is deliberately narrow. No community features. No sharing. No audience. No AI ghostwriting the response. The point is that the user writes, in their own voice, under mild time pressure, every single day. The app tracks streaks, word counts, vocabulary growth, device usage, and session history. Over weeks, this compounds into a measurable improvement in voice and fluency.

10-minute timed sessions
A countdown timer removes decision paralysis. The user cannot overthink. They press start and write until the clock stops.
One curated quote per day
A new literary quote each morning serves as the spark. Users respond to it, push against it, or use it as a point of departure.
Built-in rhetoric and vocabulary
Each prompt includes three techniques -- rhetorical devices like chiasmus, sonic devices like assonance, or vocabulary words like ephemeral. Users log which ones they used.
Progress tracking
Streaks, word count averages, session history, vocabulary range growth, and a weekly report card show users that they are actually improving.
OCR handwriting scan
Users who prefer pen and paper can photograph their handwritten session. OCR converts it to digital text instantly, preserving it in their history.
Built-in dictionary
Look up any word without leaving the app. Definitions, part of speech, example sentences, and synonyms appear inline so flow is never broken.

What Pensee is not. It is not a notes app, a journal with prompts, a grammar checker, or an AI writing assistant. It does not help users produce content for an audience. It helps them develop a voice for themselves. This distinction matters in every post: the app is positioned against AI writing tools, not alongside them.

Target audience. The posts speak to three overlapping groups:

Primary
The lapsed writer
Someone who used to write and has drifted. A former English student, a person who kept journals in their teens and stopped. They believe they have lost something. Pensee positions itself as the way to get it back.
Secondary
The AI-resistant
Someone who has noticed that AI is eroding their ability to think in sentences. Professionals, students, or knowledge workers who feel vaguely unease about what is being outsourced. The anti-AI messaging speaks directly to them.
Tertiary
The ambitious student
A high schooler or undergraduate who wants to write better. Interested in rhetorical devices, vocabulary, technique. The literary and educational angle -- devices, annotation, etymology -- is for them.

The emotional register. Pensee is warm but not cheerful. Serious but not severe. Literary without being pretentious. The posts should feel like a well-worn desk, a page of notes in good handwriting, a quote that stays with you. They should never feel like a productivity app, a self-help product, or a Silicon Valley startup.

04
Texture
Blob Gradients + Noise

Every post has both a noise overlay and at least one blob gradient. Together they give the flat HTML surfaces a handmade, press-printed quality. Neither element is decorative -- they are structural to the visual identity.

Blob 1: warm peach, opacity .09-.13, blur 65-100px Blob 2: rust, opacity .1-.13, blur 75-100px

Blob rules. Always position blobs so they bleed off at least one edge -- they should never float visibly inside the frame. Use filter: blur(65px) minimum; anything sharper looks digital rather than organic. On dark posts, keep blob opacity between 0.07 and 0.13. On light posts, raise it to 0.4-0.7 since the pale background needs more warmth to read as non-sterile.

Blob pattern -- dark post (1-2 blobs)
/* Blob always positioned to bleed off an edge */ .b1 { position: absolute; width: 340px; height: 340px; border-radius: 50%; background: #fadac1; /* warm peach */ filter: blur(70px); opacity: .12; /* 0.07-0.13 on dark */ top: -80px; right: -80px; /* bleed off corner */ pointer-events: none; }

Noise overlay. Applied to every post as the topmost layer. It subtly breaks up flat areas and makes the composition feel physically printed rather than digitally generated. Use opacity: .035 as the default; reduce to .02 on light posts where it could muddy fine white-card details.

Structure
Layout Patterns

Every post is 480 x 600 px at display size (4:5 Instagram ratio), exported at 2x pixel ratio to produce a 960 x 1200 px PNG. All content lives in a .body div with consistent padding. The brand strip is always absolutely positioned at the bottom.

Post shell -- identical on every post
/* Outer container -- always 480x600, overflow hidden */ .post { width: 480px; height: 600px; position: relative; overflow: hidden; } /* Body: z-index 1, always above blobs (z-index 0) and below noise (z-index 10) */ .body { position: relative; z-index: 1; padding: 44px 44px 76px; /* 76px bottom = clears brand strip */ height: 100%; display: flex; flex-direction: column; }

Padding conventions. Standard body padding is 44px on all sides except bottom, which is 76px to clear the brand strip. More minimal or editorial posts may use 48px 48px 76px or 52px 48px 76px. Never go below 40px on any side.

Voice + Tone
Copy Rules

The copy tone is literary, honest, and direct. It respects the reader's intelligence. It does not oversell. It avoids the inflated enthusiasm of typical app marketing.

RuleDetail
Never Em dashes Use a full stop, a comma, or rewrite the sentence. Em dashes create visual noise and feel informal. Use a plain hyphen only in compound modifiers.
Never Emoji Do not use emoji in post content. They are visual orphans in a literary aesthetic. Captions (Instagram text, not the image) may use them sparingly if the brief requires it.
Do Lucide icons When a visual indicator is needed (cameras, clocks, pen, chart), use a Lucide SVG icon at the correct size. Never a Unicode character as a substitute.
Do Present tense Write in present tense. "Pensee gives you..." not "Pensee will give you..."
Do Second person Address the reader directly: "You write for you." "Your voice matters." Avoid the third-person product voice.
Never Exclamation marks The tone is quiet confidence. Exclamation marks undermine it. Let the idea carry the energy.
Never Ellipsis (...) for trailing off End sentences firmly. An ellipsis suggests uncertainty. The copy does not trail off.
Do Short sentences Eyebrow lines: 2-4 words. Headlines: 5-12 words across 1-2 lines. Sub-text: max 2 sentences, each under 20 words.
Do Period after headline Headlines consistently end with a full stop. "Be the one that remembers." not "Be the one that remembers"
Do Italicise the key word In Playfair headlines, the word carrying the most meaning takes the italic + rust treatment. One word per headline, not a phrase.

Eyebrow formulas. Eyebrows orient the reader to the post's category. Use these established patterns:

Eyebrow examples by category
Feature spotlight -- "Feature spotlight" Writing concept -- "A thought worth sitting with" Data / stats -- "Your writing over time" Prompt / quote -- "Today's prompt" How-to -- "The honest breakdown" Etymology -- leave blank or use language of origin Milestone -- "End of week review" / "Build the habit"

Strategy
What Makes a Good Post: Content

A good Pensee post teaches, provokes, or reveals something true -- then connects that back to the app without overselling. The content never feels like advertising. It feels like something worth reading on its own, and only then does the reader notice it is from a brand.

Post content categories. Every post belongs to one of six types. A healthy content calendar rotates across all six. No more than two posts of the same type should run back to back.

Anti-AI / Anti-outsourcing Dark
Examples: P1, P2, P9, P11, P13, P18
The app's core argument. AI is eroding writing ability and voice. These posts name the problem clearly, often with a contrast (AI vs. you) or a stark statement. They should feel like something a smart person said, not like an ad. The tone is firm, not alarmist.
Feature spotlight Light
Examples: P3, P7, P10, P14, P19, P25
Shows a specific app feature through a realistic mockup or UI representation. The mockup must look real: real vocabulary words, real timestamps, real session data, real literary quotes. Generic placeholder text destroys credibility. Always name the feature clearly in the eyebrow or heading.
Literary quote / Prompt showcase Both
Examples: P8, P17, P20
A single great quote from a literary source, given space to breathe. The frame connects it to the app: this is the kind of prompt Pensee gives you, or this is why writing matters. The quote must be genuinely good. Do not use motivational aphorisms or business quotes. Aurelius, Plath, Didion, Atwood, Morrison.
Vocabulary and technique education Both
Examples: P4, P6, P15, P24
Teaches the audience something about language: a rhetorical device, a vocabulary word, an etymology, an annotation technique. These posts attract the audience that is genuinely curious about writing craft. They should not feel like textbook definitions -- use literary examples, real sentences, human explanations.
Habit and streak Light
Examples: P5, P12, P16, P21, P22, P23
Speaks to consistency, repetition, and the compound effect of daily practice. Uses real-looking stats, milestones, or writer trivia to make the case that showing up daily is the whole game. The stat numbers must be plausible and specific -- "267 words average" feels real; "250+ words" feels invented.
Brand and philosophy Dark
Examples: P6, P13, P23
The most editorial post type. A statement, a cascade, a big idea given typographic space. No feature demo, no data, no mockup. Just the argument. These work best when the writing is strong enough to stand on its own. Rarely use this type -- it has the most impact when it is not overused.

Content checklist. Before finalising any post, verify the following:

One clear idea per post
A good post makes exactly one argument or demonstrates exactly one thing. If you can summarise the post in one sentence, it is focused enough. If you need two sentences, it is trying to do too much.
The copy could stand without the brand
Cover the brand strip and ask: is this interesting on its own? A post that is only interesting because it advertises the app is a weak post. The best posts are things a thoughtful person would save or share regardless of the brand.
The mockup data is specific and real-looking
Any feature demo must use real vocabulary words (ephemeral, liminal, equanimity), real literary quotes with correct attribution, real-looking word counts (291 not 300), and real timestamps (07:24, not 10:00). Vague placeholder data signals that nobody tested the concept.
The headline earns its key word
The italicised rust word in the headline is the most important word in the post. It should be the one word that, if removed, collapses the meaning. "Be the one that remembers." The italic lands because the whole post builds to it.
The app connection is earned, not forced
The bridge to Pensee comes after the idea has been established, not before. Do not open with the app. Open with the insight, the quote, the device, the data. Let the reader nod. Then show them that Pensee is the answer to the problem you just described.
Generic writing advice without a hook
"Write every day." "Your voice matters." "Ten minutes is all it takes." These are fine as supporting lines but they are not post ideas. Every post needs a specific angle: a named writer, a specific device, a provocative contrast, a real data point. Generality is invisible on a feed.
More than one feature per post
Feature spotlight posts show one feature. Trying to mention the timer, the dictionary, and the OCR scanner in the same post produces a confused layout and a weak argument. Each feature deserves its own post.
Motivational quotes from business or self-help
The quote library is strictly literary. Marcus Aurelius, Toni Morrison, Joan Didion, Sylvia Plath, Haruki Murakami, Margaret Atwood. Not Gary Vaynerchuk, not Simon Sinek, not anonymous. The literary credibility of the quotes is core to the brand.
Repeating the same post type back to back
Two dark editorial posts in a row dilutes both. Two feature spotlights in a row feels like a product demo reel. The feed should alternate mode (dark/light) and rotate type across the six categories.

Quality Control
What Makes a Good Post: Visuals

A technically correct post uses the right colours, fonts, and spacing. A visually good post goes further: it has spatial balance, textural warmth, a clear focal point, and enough negative space that the composition breathes. These qualities are harder to specify but easy to see.

The four visual qualities of a strong post.

Spatial balance

The post is not top-heavy or bottom-heavy. The headline zone and content zone occupy their proportional thirds. If the headline runs four lines, the content zone needs to shrink. If there is a single big typographic statement, it should be centred or weighted slightly above centre, not drifting to a corner.

Textural warmth

The post looks physically made, not digitally generated. This comes from three things working together: the noise overlay breaking up flat areas, the blob gradients adding ambient light and warmth, and the Playfair Display giving the type a printed quality. If any one of these is missing, the post looks sterile.

Clear focal point

There is one thing the eye lands on first. On dark posts this is usually the main headline. On feature posts it is the card or UI element. On typographic posts it is the largest or brightest text. If two elements compete for primary attention, one of them needs to recede: reduce its size, lower its opacity, or remove it.

Negative space

The post is not full. The padding system (40-44px on sides, 76px bottom) exists precisely to keep the content away from the edges. Within the content zone, avoid filling every pixel. A list with four items and breathing room between rows is better than six items crammed together. White space signals confidence.

Common visual failure modes.

Too many font sizes

Using five different sizes in one post fragments the hierarchy. Each post should have at most three size steps: the headline, the supporting text, and the micro labels. Everything else should collapse into one of these three levels.

Grey text on dark background

Any rgba opacity below 0.55 on dark posts is illegible on a phone screen in daylight. The background is near-black; anything that is not warm white at reasonable opacity disappears. Always check against the opacity ladder in Section 01.

Blobs that do not bleed off the edge

A blob that is fully contained within the frame looks like a spotlight, not an ambient glow. Every blob must be positioned so that at minimum one of its edges is cut off by the post boundary. This is what creates the sense of warmth coming from beyond the frame.

Cards that are too small to read

Feature mockup cards need enough vertical space to render their content at readable font sizes. The minimum text size inside a card is 10px for labels, 11px for body, 12px for definitions. If a card tries to show too much, cut items until it can breathe -- never shrink the text.

Consecutive same-mode posts

Two dark posts in a row is fine occasionally. Three is a pattern. Four or more in a row makes the feed feel heavy and monotonous. The light posts are not just visual relief -- they are structurally important to the rhythm. Feature spotlights are almost always light; plan them to break up dark editorial runs.

Missing noise or missing blobs

A post without the noise overlay has flat colour fields that look like a Canva template. A post without blobs looks clinical. Both elements are mandatory on every post. The noise div must have z-index 10 and pointer-events none. The blobs must be position:absolute with pointer-events none so they do not interfere with layout.

The feed as a whole. Posts are not experienced one at a time. A visitor who arrives at the Pensee profile sees a grid. The grid should alternate dark and light. It should show variety in layout type (not all text, not all cards). It should never have two posts that look like twins -- same background mode, same layout, same approximate amount of text.

Before adding a new post to the batch, check it against the last two posts. It should differ in at least two of these dimensions: background mode, layout type, content category, and dominant visual element (headline vs. card vs. data vs. quote).

Visual self-assessment. Print or screenshot the post and ask these five questions. If any answer is no, revise before exporting.

Can I read every word at arm's length?
Hold the screen at normal reading distance. If any text requires squinting, it is too small or too low in opacity. The smallest text on the post (labels, chips, micro-copy) should still be comfortably legible.
Does the post have a single thing I look at first?
Cover everything except the element you intended to be the focal point. Does the rest of the post support and frame it? If another element keeps drawing attention, reconsider the hierarchy.
Does it feel warm, not cold?
The palette is deliberately warm. If the post looks neutral or grey, check that the blobs are in place and bleeding off the edges, that the noise overlay is present, and that no text colours have drifted into neutral grey rather than the warm off-white family.
Does it look different from the previous two posts?
Check background mode, layout type, and approximate visual density. At least two of these three should differ from the preceding posts in the batch.
Would someone save this even without knowing what Pensee is?
The best posts are things the audience finds independently useful -- a quote, a technique, a thought, a piece of data. If the post only makes sense as an ad, it is weaker than it should be. Aim for posts that earn their own attention before earning attention for the brand.
